\section{Computational Methodology}

\subsection{Lattice Boltzmann Method and Solver Formulation}
Unlike the Finite Volume Method (FVM), which discretizes the macroscopic Navier-Stokes equations and requires computationally expensive procedures to resolve pressure-velocity coupling, the Lattice Boltzmann Method (LBM) operates at the mesoscopic scale. It solves the discrete Boltzmann transport equation, recovering macroscopic hydrodynamics via the Chapman-Enskog expansion \cite{chen1998lattice}. This kinetic approach offers explicit time integration, exact linear advection, and local collision rules, making it highly efficient for handling the complex boundaries inherent to porous media. The spatial domain is discretized into a uniform global Cartesian mesh. The velocity space at each node is quantized using the D2Q9 lattice model, which defines nine discrete velocity vectors $\mathbf{c}_i$: one rest particle, four cardinal, and four diagonal directions \cite{qian1992lattice}. This specific lattice provides the necessary symmetry and isotropy to correctly recover the full Navier-Stokes stress tensor in two dimensions.

The evolution of the system is governed by the discrete Lattice Boltzmann equation employing the single-relaxation-time Bhatnagar-Gross-Krook (BGK) collision operator \cite{bhatnagar1954model}. To accurately couple the phase-field model and introduce macroscopic forces into the mesoscopic distributions, an exact forcing term $S_i$ \cite{guo2002discrete} is utilized:
\begin{equation}
    f_i(\mathbf{x} + \mathbf{c}_i \Delta t, t + \Delta t) = f_i(\mathbf{x}, t) - \frac{1}{\tau(\phi)} \left[ f_i(\mathbf{x}, t) - f_i^{eq}(\mathbf{x}, t) \right] + S_i \Delta t
\end{equation}
where $f_i$ are the density distribution functions, $\mathbf{c}_i$ are the discrete lattice velocities, and $\tau$ is the relaxation time. Computationally, $\tau$ is linearly interpolated across the fluid interface based on the local phase fraction $\phi$. This interpolation ensures a smooth transition of kinematic viscosity between the distinct fluid phases, avoiding numerical instabilities associated with sharp gradients. \subsection{Cahn-Hilliard Solver: Spatial Discretization and Temporal Substepping}

The order parameter $\phi$ whose theoretical formulation was established in the Phase-Field Modeling section is evolved by a dedicated finite-difference module that runs alongside the LBM hydrodynamic kernel. Because the advective Cahn-Hilliard equation
\begin{equation}
    \frac{\partial \phi}{\partial t} + \mathbf{u} \cdot \nabla \phi = M\,\nabla^2 \mu_c, \qquad \mu_c = 4\beta\,\phi(\phi^2 - 1) - \kappa\,\nabla^2 \phi,
\end{equation}
is fourth-order in $\phi$, a one-shot discretization would require a wide biharmonic stencil. Instead, the implementation splits the update into two coupled second-order passes — first computing the chemical potential field $\mu_c$ over the entire domain, then advancing $\phi$ using $\nabla^2 \mu_c$ — which reduces every stencil to five points and makes the algorithm naturally local.

\subsubsection{Five-Point Isotropic Stencil}
All spatial derivatives are evaluated on the same uniform Cartesian grid used by the LBM kernel, with lattice spacing $\Delta x = 1$ in lattice units. The Laplacian of any scalar field $\psi$ is approximated by the standard five-point stencil:
\begin{equation}
    \nabla^2 \psi(y, x) \approx \psi(y, x{+}1) + \psi(y, x{-}1) + \psi(y{+}1, x) + \psi(y{-}1, x) - 4\,\psi(y, x).
\end{equation}
The first spatial derivatives entering the advective term $\mathbf{u} \cdot \nabla \phi$ are computed using second-order centred differences, e.g.\ $\partial_x \phi \approx [\phi(y, x{+}1) - \phi(y, x{-}1)]/2$. The five-point Laplacian carries a small leading-order anisotropy, but this is acceptable here because the Cahn-Hilliard relaxation acts on a thermodynamic timescale much shorter than the hydrodynamic timescale, smoothing out grid-induced artefacts. By contrast, the macroscopic force computation in the LBM kernel uses the more demanding 9-point isotropic stencil, since spurious anisotropic currents at the interface would directly contaminate the momentum balance.

\subsubsection{Boundary Conditions}
The boundary treatment of the order parameter is fully consistent with the boundary conditions imposed by the LBM kernel. The transverse boundaries ($y = 0$ and $y = N_y - 1$) are always treated as periodic, mirroring the spanwise periodicity of the LBM mesh. The longitudinal boundaries depend on the simulation mode:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Inlet ($x = 0$, injection mode):} a Dirichlet condition $\phi = +1$ is enforced, representing the continuous injection of the pure invading phase. The condition is reapplied both before computing $\mu_c$ and after the temporal update, to prevent the explicit Euler step from disturbing the boundary value through the centred-difference advective stencil.
    \item \textbf{Outlet ($x = N_x - 1$):} a homogeneous Neumann condition $\partial_x \phi = 0$ is enforced by copying the value from the adjacent internal node, $\phi_{N_x-1} = \phi_{N_x-2}$. This allows the interface to be advected smoothly out of the computational domain without spurious reflection.
    \item \textbf{Chemical potential $\mu_c$ at both $x$-boundaries:} homogeneous Neumann ($\partial_x \mu_c = 0$). Physically, this nullifies the diffusive flux $M \nabla \mu_c$ across the longitudinal boundaries — at the inlet, the phase composition is dictated by the injection Dirichlet condition, not by diffusion; at the outlet, the only allowed mass flux is the advective sweep by the velocity field.
\end{itemize}
For fully periodic configurations (e.g.\ spatially periodic linear-stability test cases), both $x$-boundaries are treated periodically through modular indexing, identical to the $y$ treatment.

\subsubsection{Explicit Euler Update and Numerical Safeguards}
After the chemical potential field has been finalised, the order parameter is advanced through an explicit first-order Euler scheme:
\begin{equation}
    \phi^{n+1}(y, x) = \phi^n(y, x) + \Delta t_{CH}\left[ M\,\nabla^2 \mu_c - \left(u_x\,\partial_x \phi + u_y\,\partial_y \phi\right) \right].
\end{equation}
The velocity field $(u_x, u_y)$ here is the physical velocity recovered from the Guo forcing scheme described below. To prevent unphysical overshoots of the order parameter beyond the bulk equilibrium values $\phi = \pm 1$ — overshoots that the quartic bulk term of $\mathcal{F}$ can amplify into catastrophic numerical blow-up — the updated values are clamped to the physical interval $\phi^{n+1} \in [-1, +1]$ before being stored. In well-resolved simulations (sufficient interface thickness and properly calibrated mobility), the clamp is essentially never active and the associated mass error is negligible; the clamp is retained as a numerical safety net for transient instabilities, e.g.\ during the start-up phase or near very high mobility-ratio interfaces.

The update is implemented through a double-buffer pattern (\texttt{phi\_in}, \texttt{phi\_out}) so that the right-hand side at each grid point is evaluated using the field $\phi^n$ everywhere, never with partially updated values from earlier in the sweep. The buffer pointers are swapped externally between substeps, avoiding any in-place memory hazard.

\subsubsection{Temporal Substepping and CFL Constraint}
The Cahn-Hilliard equation is fourth-order in space, so the explicit-Euler stability bound scales as $(\Delta x)^4$:
\begin{equation}
    \Delta t_{CH} \lesssim \frac{(\Delta x)^4}{4\,M\,\kappa},
\end{equation}
which is considerably more restrictive than the convective CFL of the LBM kernel ($\Delta t_{LBM} \lesssim \Delta x / c_s$). To preserve the natural unit time step of the LBM while honouring the Cahn-Hilliard stability bound, the solver is invoked $N_{sub}$ times per LBM step, with reduced substep $\Delta t_{CH} = \Delta t_{LBM} / N_{sub}$. The number of substeps is chosen at runtime such that the diffusive CFL is satisfied with a safety margin, conventionally targeting $M\,\kappa\,\Delta t_{CH} / (\Delta x)^4 \leq 0.1$. For the simulations presented in this work, typical values fall in the range $N_{sub} \in [5, 20]$, with larger values required when $\sigma$ (and therefore the product $\kappa\beta$) is increased to suppress numerical mixing at high mobility ratio.

\subsubsection{Parallelization and Coupling}
The substep routine is compiled by Numba with \texttt{@njit(parallel=True, cache=True)}, and the outer loop over transverse rows ($y$) is parallelised using \texttt{prange}. Because the five-point stencil is local and the temporal update writes only to \texttt{phi\_out}, there are no inter-row data dependencies and the parallel reduction is embarrassingly parallel. The chemical-potential computation and the temporal update are kept in separate parallel passes because the second pass reads $\mu_c$ at the four nearest neighbours and therefore requires the $\mu_c$ field to be fully populated across all rows before any value of $\phi^{n+1}$ is computed. This two-phase structure is the algorithmic image of the second-order operator splitting of the original fourth-order equation.

At the end of each LBM step, the updated phase field $\phi$ is consumed by three physically distinct couplings in the next iteration: the heterogeneous magnetic susceptibility $\chi(\phi)$ used by the Poisson solver, the local permeability $K(\phi)$ used by the Guo forcing scheme, and the interpolated kinematic viscosity $\nu(\phi)$ entering the BGK relaxation time. The capillary body force $\mathbf{F}_s = \mu_c \nabla \phi$ is reassembled inside the LBM kernel from the latest $\phi$ field rather than being passed in from the Cahn-Hilliard module, so that all three macroscopic forces ($\mathbf{F}_s$, $\mathbf{F}_m$, and the Darcy drag) act on the same instantaneous phase configuration.

\subsection{External Forcing: Interfacial and Magnetic Couplings}
The total macroscopic body force $\mathbf{F} = \mathbf{F}_s + \mathbf{F}_m$ acts as a source term for the momentum update at each discrete time step.

The isotropic capillary force (Korteweg stress) is recovered directly from the chemical potential gradient of the Cahn-Hilliard phase-field model \cite{cahn1958free}:
\begin{equation}
    \mathbf{F}_s = \mu \nabla \phi = \left[ 4\beta\phi(\phi^2-1) - \kappa\nabla^2\phi \right] \nabla \phi
\end{equation}

The magnetic  force (Kelvin force) arises from heterogeneities in the magnetic susceptibility $\chi(\phi)$ subjected to an external scalar potential field $\psi$. To suppress grid-dependent truncation errors in the numerical implementation, this force is computed via tensorial decomposition:
\begin{equation}
    \mathbf{F}_m = \chi \left( \frac{\partial \psi}{\partial x} \nabla \frac{\partial \psi}{\partial x} + \frac{\partial \psi}{\partial y} \nabla \frac{\partial \psi}{\partial y} \right)
\end{equation}
All spatial derivatives and Laplacians required to compute these interfacial and magnetic forces are evaluated using 9-point isotropic finite difference schemes. This approach mitigates the intrinsic geometric anisotropy of the Cartesian grid and improves numerical stability near moving interfaces.

\subsection{Darcy Resistance and Kinematic Recovery (Guo Scheme)}
To incorporate the Darcy-Brinkman resistance tensor representing a porous medium with permeability $K$, the physical fluid velocity $\mathbf{u}_{phys}$ must be decoupled from the raw first-order statistical moment. The implementation adopts the Guo forcing scheme \cite{guo2002discrete}, which implicitly recovers the true physical velocity by analytically solving for the drag parameter $\sigma = \nu/K$:
\begin{equation}
    \mathbf{u}_{phys} = \frac{\sum f_i \mathbf{c}_i + \frac{\Delta t}{2} (\mathbf{F}_s + \mathbf{F}_m)}{1 + \frac{\Delta t}{2} \sigma}
\end{equation}
Consequently, the effective total force used to compute the discrete source term $S_i$ in the evolution equation is dynamically adjusted by subtracting the viscous pressure drop induced by the solid matrix: $\mathbf{F}_{total} = (\mathbf{F}_s + \mathbf{F}_m) - \sigma \rho \mathbf{u}_{phys}$.

\subsection{Boundary Conditions}
The streaming step advects post-collision populations to adjacent nodes. At the longitudinal solid boundaries ($y=0$ and $y=N_y-1$), the no-slip condition is enforced via the half-way bounce-back scheme \cite{zou1997analytical}. Algorithmically, this reflects the incoming distribution functions strictly back to their origin node ($\mathbf{c}_{\bar{i}} = -\mathbf{c}_i$), ensuring exact mass conservation at the walls.

At the transverse open boundaries, asymmetric conditions are implemented to simulate a semi-infinite flow-driven channel:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Inlet ($x=0$)}: A constant velocity Dirichlet condition is applied. Missing incoming populations are reconstructed using the equilibrium distribution $f_i^{eq}$ defined by the prescribed velocity $\mathbf{u} = (U_{in}, 0)$. To prevent unphysical pressure build-up and maintain consistency with the internal Darcy resistance field, the local macroscopic density is not hard-coded; instead, it is backward-extrapolated from the first internal fluid node ($\rho = \rho_{x=1}$).
    \item \textbf{Outlet ($x=N_x-1$)}: A Neumann-type convective boundary condition is applied to allow the smooth exit of interfacial perturbations. The code implements a second-order spatial extrapolation for the populations ($f_i^{N_x-1} = 2f_i^{N_x-2} - f_i^{N_x-3}$). This numerical approach effectively nullifies the second derivatives at the boundary, mitigating spurious mass and acoustic reflections back into the computational domain.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Algorithm Structure and Main Loop}
The computational implementation strictly follows the standard LBM algorithm structure. After initializing the macroscopic fields ($\rho, \mathbf{u}, \phi$) and establishing the initial equilibrium distributions, the main temporal loop executes the following sequential steps at each iteration:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Macroscopic and Forcing Calculation}: The interfacial variables and external forces ($\mathbf{F}_s, \mathbf{F}_m$) are evaluated using spatial finite differences. The physical velocity $\mathbf{u}_{phys}$ is subsequently recovered.
    \item \textbf{Collision Step}: The local equilibrium distribution $f_i^{eq}$ and source term $S_i$ are computed. The distributions $f_i$ are then relaxed locally according to the BGK operator.
    \item \textbf{Streaming Step}: The post-collision populations are advected linearly to their respective neighboring nodes along the $\mathbf{c}_i$ directions.
    \item \textbf{Boundary Conditions}: The bounce-back and open boundary rules are applied to reconstruct unknown incoming populations at the domain edges.
    \item \textbf{Macroscopic Update}: The fluid density $\rho$ and phase fraction $\phi$ are updated via the zeroth-order moments of their respective distributions, advancing the system to $t + \Delta t$.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Darcy-Brinkman Regime and Permeability Validation}

\subsection{Guo Forcing Scheme and Kinematic Synchronization}
In porous media simulations, the solid matrix induces a macroscopic drag force $\mathbf{F}_d = -\frac{\nu}{K} \rho \mathbf{u}$. Incorporating this velocity-dependent resistance directly into the standard Lattice Boltzmann equation introduces numerical instability and breaks the second-order spatial accuracy of the collision operator. 

To resolve this, the implementation utilizes the exact forcing scheme proposed by Guo et al. \cite{guo2002discrete}. This method analytically decouples the true physical velocity $\mathbf{u}$ from the macroscopic force computation by solving the implicit momentum balance:
\begin{equation}
    \mathbf{u} = \frac{\sum f_i \mathbf{c}_i + \frac{\Delta t}{2} \mathbf{F}_{ext}}{\rho \left(1 + \frac{\Delta t}{2} \frac{\nu}{K}\right)}
\end{equation}
where $\mathbf{F}_{ext}$ encapsulates all explicit external body forces (e.g., capillary or magnetic forces) and $K$ is the intrinsic permeability. 

This formulation guarantees algorithmic synchronization with normal free-flow regions. In zones without porous structures ($K \to \infty$), the structural resistance denominator converges to unity ($\sigma = \nu/K \to 0$). Consequently, the system recovers standard Navier-Stokes hydrodynamics smoothly and continuously, eliminating the need for conditional logic (branching) at fluid-porous interfaces. The numerical validation of the porous resistance was conducted in a $200 \times 50$ lattice domain. The flow was driven by a constant kinematic inlet ($U_{in} = 0.01$ lu) and constrained by a strict thermodynamic Dirichlet boundary at the outlet ($\rho_{out} = 1.0$). To rigorously evaluate the permeability recovery, it is mandatory to eliminate spurious kinetic artifacts generated by non-equilibrium population reconstructions at the open boundaries. Therefore, the hydrodynamic variables were extracted exclusively from the channel centerline ($y = N_y/2$) within a spatially truncated, fully developed ``bulk region'' defined between $x = 0.25N_x$ and $x = 0.75N_x$. 

The thermodynamic pressure $P$ was computed directly from the local density via the LBM isothermal equation of state ($P = c_s^2 \rho$). For a homogeneous porous medium, Darcy's Law dictates that the longitudinal pressure profile must exhibit a strictly linear drop. The macroscopic pressure gradient $\nabla P$ was extracted numerically via a linear least-squares regression applied to the centerline pressure nodes within the bulk region.

Subsequently, the algorithm's recovered numerical permeability ($K_{num}$) was calculated by inverting Darcy's equation:
\begin{equation}
    K_{num} = \frac{\nu \bar{\rho} \bar{u}_x}{-\nabla P}
\end{equation}
where $\bar{\rho}$ and $\bar{u}_x$ are the spatially averaged density and longitudinal velocity across the bulk region.
